Lan Studio began as a simple question: why do people form emotional connections with certain products? I started by selling handmade home decor at local markets, spending weekends not just making sales, but having conversations. I learned that customers weren't buying products—they were buying stories, craftsmanship, and the promise of a home that felt more like them.

As the business grew from market stalls to e-commerce, I became the entire product team: researcher, designer, and business strategist. Every customer complaint became a usability study. Every return was a failed user journey. Every five-star review revealed what actually resonated with people. I wasn't just A/B testing buttons—I was testing business hypotheses in real time with real money on the line.

What This Experience Taught Me About Product Strategy

1

Customer Development is Everything

I learned to treat every customer interaction as qualitative research. The woman who spent 20 minutes explaining why she chose a specific ceramic vase taught me more about our value proposition than any survey could. This taught me that the best insights often come from listening to what people do, not just what they say.

2

Design Decisions Have Real Business Consequences

When I simplified the product photography to show items in realistic home settings (instead of perfect studio shots), conversion rates increased by 30%. This wasn't just a design preference—it was a business decision that directly impacted revenue. I learned to make design choices based on business outcomes, not just aesthetics.

3

Building Beats Theorizing

I stopped guessing what customers wanted and started building minimum viable products. The fastest way to validate an idea wasn't another focus group—it was putting a prototype in front of real customers and seeing if they'd actually pay for it. This 'build-measure-learn' mentality became my default approach to product development.
